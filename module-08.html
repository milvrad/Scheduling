Module 08 • Specialty overlays

🧠 Specialty Workflows: Neuro, Fluoro, Peds, NM/PET, Tumor Boards, Admin

Apply specialty-specific overlays that affect the schedule after basic daily and weekly structure is understood.

WhatWhyWhat to useHow to use itSelf-check
Module progress:

Learning objectives

  • Describe the weekly Neuro Fluoro target and early Neuro coverage trigger.
  • Explain how Peds and NM/PET require WebExchange and count checks.
  • Recognize tumor board and admin-day impacts on placement.
  • Identify which specialty workflow items require notes or source checks before scheduling.

Why this matters

  • Specialty workflows are high-risk because they affect specific readers, sites, times, and public-facing call listings.
  • Tumor boards and admin days may make a radiologist unavailable for certain assignments even when they appear otherwise available.

What to use

  • Neuro Rads Fluoro Weekly Schedule to equalize PF/Fluoro/H.O. assignments.
  • QGenda notes for early Neuro coverage and special assignments.
  • WebExchange for Peds and NM call checks.
  • SharePoint/Tumor Board files and current tumor board rad list.
  • Residency/Admin tracking workbook and current meeting calendar.
  • PET count logic separating brain studies from the total.

How to use it

  1. For Neuro, assign two half-days of Fluoro each week where possible and use the Neuro Rads Fluoro Weekly Schedule to balance assignments.
  2. When the early Neuro trigger applies, schedule the early Neuro radiologist from home and add the correct weekday or weekend note.
  3. Check Peds and NM call in WebExchange to confirm no unapproved changes were made.
  4. Track PET brain studies separately and exclude them from the total count.
  5. Print or review monthly tumor board schedules before assigning radiologists.
  6. Place tumor board notes or assignments in QGenda when needed to prevent inappropriate placement.
  7. Use the current admin/residency tracking resource before entering admin days.
  8. Re-check specialty assignments before publication because they are often site-, modality-, or physician-specific.
